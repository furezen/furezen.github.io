Why Social Media Impressions Are Worthless

by Claire Repass
February 16, 2015  | 

Why do you use social media for your events?

No really, why?

If your default reaction was, “because everyone does it” or “because we’re supposed to,” you should cancel all of your accounts. Shut them down. Right now. If you don’t know why you’re doing something—if you don’t have data that you measure your social media efforts on—, then you are tweeting, posting or uploading into a void, and doing your team, your attendees, and yourself a disservice by wasting their time. Keep reading.

On the other hand, if your reaction was, “I use social media to drive website traffic, raise awareness for my event, fortify our messaging and to increase registration that directly impacts my company/client’s bottom line,” then bravo! You probably don’t need to read the rest of this post, but you should anyway to let me know if I missed anything.

One of my favorite quotes about social media marketing goes like this: “It’s about action, not eyeballs.” Who cares if you got 20,000 impressions? Who cares if you got 20 million impressions? An impression is merely the number of times something is seen. Sure, some would respond that impressions drive brand awareness – that every time I see an ad for your event it puts it back into the forefront of my mind. It’s a fair argument, but when you consider that the average person is exposed to 300-700 marketing messages a day, it’s safe to say that an “impression” will be far from lasting.

So if not impressions, what are you looking for?

Engagement. Attendees are no longer passive in anything they do or expect, and mobile devices play a key role in disseminating their findings. According to the Pew Research Center, 74% of adults use social media networks, and of those, 41% share content they discover with their social networks. From sharing to commenting to retweeting – encouraging their engagement with your content is where your efforts should be focused to get the highest ROI on the time you spend strategizing, creating and launching your social media content.

With over 800 social media apps on the market, where should you begin to solicit engagement? Great news, 800 is a noisy group with only a handful of decidedly worthwhile solutions to apply your efforts towards. Flurry released a study on 2014 mobile trends that found the average American spends 162 minutes per day on their devices, and for the sake of this post, I’ve extracted the time spent on social media: 17% Facebook, 1.5% Twitter, and 4% on YouTube. Instagram, Pinterest and LinkedIn round out the group, according to Pew. Harness these sites, in this order, to achieve maximum results with the least amount of effort.

Your goal should be to provide opportunities for your attendees to both engage with and experience your brand. That in turn will connect them at a deeper level to your team, their peers, the industry, and your event. “Every event organizer has an ultimate goal of creating a better experience for their attendees, sponsors, guest speakers and exhibitors,” says Alicia Tillman, Vice President of Deal Consulting and Business Services for American Express Global Business Travel. “Those participating in the event want to connect with as many as people as possible throughout the lifecycle of a program. In today’s environment the best way to achieve these goals is through technology.”

Use your paid marketing dollars on social media to engage your attendees through such measures as gated content downloads (email acquisition for your sales team), social media competitions (if you share, you’re entered to win X), community discussions (be the connector for like-minded individuals), etc. The more you expose your target attendees to your brand in a memorable, interactive way, the more return you’ll get. It’s a simple principle: an engaged audience is more valuable than a passive audience. Help your attendees be your brand ambassadors by providing content that is compelling, shareable, and exciting.
