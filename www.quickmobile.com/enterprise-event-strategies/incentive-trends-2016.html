Incentive Trends for 2016

by Kerry J. Plowman
July 7, 2016  | 

I recently had the opportunity to sit down and chat with Roger Stotz, Chief Research Officer of the Incentive Research Foundation (IRF), and discuss trends for the incentive travel market. After having presented incentive trends for 2016 at the IRF Invitational conference in Las Vegas, he had plenty of insights to share with us.

According to Stotz, the biggest and most quantifiable trend is the overall growth of the industry. Since the IRF began tracking incentive expenditures, there has been a significant increase in both the number of people being rewarded with incentives as well as the average size of expenditure for incentives. In essence, more people are enjoying greater incentives. The growth in non-cash rewards in business is up 13% over the last 10 years.

The second major trend that Stotz noted is the demographics and expectations of the newer incentive travel recipients. Namely millennials, who are vastly more tech savvy and employ more virtual resources to become informed. They in turn, redefine luxury travel and alter the offerings typically provided in incentive packages. Millennials have essentially changed the focus of incentive travel from a traditional packaged program into a boutique experience, with a preference for unbundled programs.

The last major trend revolves around disruption. Incentive program organizers must now take into greater account the balance between safety and risk. The challenge is addressing the perception of the safety of a particular destination to attendees, their immediate family, and friends who fell upon an article on possible dangers of the area. New concerns such as political upheavals and international events create constantly evolving danger zones. Travel organizers have to essentially build a database of areas that can confidently represent an area’s safety and reassure discouraged attendees. The other happenings that demand awareness include cyber terrorism and new health concerns, such as the widely reported mosquito-borne West Nile and Zika viruses.
