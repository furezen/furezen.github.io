4 Reasons Meetings Matter

by Hannah Kaeter
April 6, 2015

Sometimes meetings get a bad rap. There’s a lot of negative press out there, and with headlines like “Meetings: Even More of a Soul-Sucking Waste of Time than You Thought,” “5 Signs Your Meetings Suck,” and “Why Meetings are Bad for You,” you may be wondering if meetings are worth it. But if planned and executed strategically, meetings can offer many valuable benefits to companies and their employees.

Here are four reasons not to believe the bad meeting hype:

1. Meetings help create a sense of community among team members

Not all meetings have to be focused on solving a problem. Meetings can and should be scheduled to keep the members of your work team in contact with one another. It is much more difficult to ask for assistance on a project from someone who feels like a stranger than it is to ask the same from someone who feels like a teammate in the office. Communication and familiarity needs to be nurtured organically, and meetings meant solely for growth within your company or team will promote cohesion and positivity.

2. Meetings provide a reliable communication platform

When something goes wrong in a business, it’s usually because somewhere along the line, a miscommunication occurred. Whether this miscommunication was between a manager and an employee, an employee and a client, or any other combination of people, it is an avoidable problem. Meeting face-to-face on a regular basis to review current projects and their progress will help avoid miscommunications and allow for questions to arise and be addressed before they become bigger issues down the road. Try implementing a regular meeting format that includes an interactive component, like a Q&A session or responding to survey questions, so you can ensure you are always hearing employees’ questions, concerns and comments.

3. Meetings lead to collaboration and problem-solving

While it might be nice to think that we can always solve problems on our own, the collective intelligence of a group can lead to better ideas and solutions than those by one individual. The unique perspective that everyone brings to the table can often help shed new light on problems, leading to more creative brainstorming sessions and outcomes. Offering a non-verbal method of idea sharing will also encourage everyone to contribute, not just the most vocal of the group.

4. Meetings promote focus on the here and now

If your job entails sitting at a computer for most of your workday, you understand how easy it is to become distracted by other people or projects. Academic studies show that office workers are interrupted, or self-interrupt, roughly every three minutes. An email pop up here, an urgent instant message from your coworker there, a phone call you weren’t expecting mid-thought – all of these daily occurrences interfere with your ability to focus on the task at-hand. Meetings bring people together around one project or purpose and force them to concentrate on the subject of that particular meeting. Eliminating these distractions with a simple meeting allows for better communication, an improved workflow, and enables your team to get more done.
