3 Ways to Create an Effective Meeting

by Claire Repass
March 13, 2015  | 

Unproductive meetings exist for 3 reasons: the wrong people in the room, a lack of preparedness/buy-in, and the omission of a responsible party to keep the meeting on track.

Learn how to fight these saboteurs to create an effective meeting:

Be strategic with your roster

In November, AirTame released a video titled, “Every Meeting Ever”, which provided inspiration for this portion of the article, solicited more than a few laughs, and made me wish that more people took the Steve Jobs approach to cutting out unnecessary attendees.

If you’re not sure what I’m referring to, there is a famous story wherein Jobs entered a meeting, sat for a few moments, noticed someone he didn’t think should be in the room, and curtly asked them to leave. In Jobs’ opinion, if your attendance didn’t add value, you shouldn’t be present.

Jeff Bezos, CEO of Amazon, shares Jobs’ thought here, but refers to it as the “Two Pizza Rule”: don’t bring more people to the table than 2 pizzas can feed (between 5 and 7 persons). His reasoning behind this is to protect progress from the idea of “groupthink” – or the influence of too many ideas and opinions on the ability to affect a decision.

Bezos nailed it with his serving size, as decision-making decreases 10% for every person in the room over 7. This means that if you have 17 people in a room, expect not to leave with any decision being made.

Identify the key stakeholders and decision makers who truly need to be present, and make sure that they all know the reason they were chosen to attend. Lack of clarity on personal purpose can be as big a detractor as AirTame’s Rambler – that person who can fit one sentence into 14 paragraphs…

Institute mandatory study hall

How many of us are guilty of the following scenario:

A meeting is added to your calendar, with the objectives clearly outlined in the body of the invite. You are asked to review it and to provide any additions to the schedule. A day before the meeting, the organizer sends a reminder and a brief overview of what will be discussed.

You don’t open the email until you’re walking into the conference room, and the organizer is left frustrated and the meeting is not effective due to lack of preparation.

We’ve all been the organizer, and we’ve all been the attendee in this situation. No one wins here, which is one of the reasons why Jeff Bezos, the CEO of Amazon, refuses to tolerate such behavior.

“In senior executive Amazon meetings, before any conversation or discussion begins, everyone sits for 30 minutes in total silence, carefully reading six-page printed memos,” reported Walter Chen. “Reading together in the meeting guarantees everyone’s undivided attention to the issues at hand.” Bezos told Fortune that he believes this act of communal reading guarantees the group’s undivided attention.

At your next meeting, block out the first 5-10 minutes for this study group-esque review time. You may not need a 6-page printed memo, but at minimum consider providing the schedule alongside a detailed memo reminding the attendees what the purpose of the meeting is, what the goals for the meeting are, and why their attendance was requested.

Assign a facilitator to oversee the discussion

A facilitator should be introduced during non-traditional meetings, wherein large decisions need to be made or new initiatives are being tackled. They will act as the guardian of the meeting by encouraging input from quieter attendees, while carefully navigating around those who tend to dominate the conversation; encourage discussion with thoughtful questions and follow-ups, and bat away any distractions that may threaten the meeting’s objectives.

Find someone with a personality, decision-making abilities, and the ability to identify a good idea and encourage attendees to drill into it.

A word of caution: make sure that the person you select for this important role is best fit to facilitate the session by asking for industry referrals and vetting candidates. The worst thing you could do would be to invest resources into someone who will add no value or does not understand your objectives for the meeting.

If your budget doesn’t allow for an outside facilitator, take a page out of EverNote CEO Phil Libin’s book and invite a member of the team who doesn’t belong to the specific department or have any background on the topic at hand. Libin says that during this intentional introduction, which they refer to as “officer training”, the “outsider” is expected to both facilitate the conversation, and learn about other departments. “They’re there to absorb what we’re talking about,” Libin says. “They’re not just spectators. They ask questions; they talk.”
