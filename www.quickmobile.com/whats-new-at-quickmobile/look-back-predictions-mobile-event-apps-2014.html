A Look Back at Our Predictions for Mobile Event Apps in 2014

by QuickMobile
November 13, 2014

Whenever you stick your neck out to predict what will happen in the future—a football game, an election, or in this case the state of mobile event apps—you put your reputation at stake. Technology is no different (in fact it may be more hazardous). But here at QuickMobile, we’re not afraid of a little risk, which is why we went public with some bold predictions about what would happen with mobile event apps in 2014. Now, nearly 12 months later, it’s time to take stock and see how we did:

Prediction #1: Enterprise-grade mobile event apps become available to everyone

Achieved

We defined ‘enterprise-grade’ as offering more scalability and reliability. This means more apps off a single platform, and more dependable uptime once an app goes live. Speaking from our own experience, we saw an explosion in sales of multi-event app platforms (which we first introduced to the event and meeting industry in 2012) as more clients recognized the value of using a single platform for several events, and for controlling spend by negotiating better deals with a single vendor. Elsewhere in the market, pretty much every major event app vendor introduced a multi-event app platform over the past year, in response to the growing demand for simpler, more scalable solutions.

We also defined ‘enterprise-grade’ as Flexible, meaning the ability to configure apps based on each event’s unique requirements. This is the hallmark of a mature technology solution, and while there are still some smaller vendors who bring a more cookie-cutter solution to the market, most are able to offer at least some degree of flexibility in their product.

Integration – the ability to connect to other business systems from registration to CRM, marketing automation and beyond. Again here, the leading vendors have opened up their solutions to enable greater integration.

By these accounts, our first prediction has come true.

Prediction #2: Social – in the right context – is more important than ever

Achieved

The media is full of examples of social networking gone wrong – both for individuals and companies. It’s a significant risk inherent in the spontaneous, always-on world of social media. But that doesn’t mean it can’t be avoided, let alone managed mid-crisis. The key here is knowing what’s appropriate. For event planners this means understanding the role the two types of social networking – public and private – play at their events. Twitter isn’t right for internal meetings, just as an enterprise social networking platform isn’t relevant to most trade show attendees. We’re happy to say that we aren’t aware of any major faux-pas when it comes to social networking and events, so we’re giving this one a passing grade.

Prediction #3: Mobile apps transform the engagement spectrum

Mostly Achieved

We brought up 4 areas of app functionality that we thought would catch on in terms of how they drive attendee engagement and app adoption. They included gamification, audience interaction, video and using the app to extend the event conversation once the curtain falls. Gamification proved its value at events like JiveWorld and IRF’s Incentive Invitational, among many others. Audience interaction through live polls, discussions and surveys have caught on big-time, with more app vendors adding these capabilities in one form or another. Video and rich media continue to be hampered by WiFi availability and bandwidth, but we should see these barriers continue to fall.

As for using the app beyond the event, we were amazed at how many of our customers began to take advantage. From pushing unique content to sharing photos, maintaining discussions and using polls and surveys, many event planners latched on to their apps’ capabilities to sustain and nurture conversations well beyond the event’s closing remarks.

Prediction #4: Mobile event apps get personal

In progress

Using mobile event apps to make events more personal is within close reach, but the reality is the industry isn’t there yet in terms of adoption. We admit we set the bar very high on this one, making it the most aspirational of all predictions for 2014. While technology like NFC and iBeacons are ripe for the picking to help elevate the personal experience of attendees, the majority of planners have yet to take advantage, for various reasons – cost and complexity at the top of the list.

Prediction #5: Event owners will leverage analytics to deepen business intelligence and validate ROI

In Progress

We scored this one as ‘In Progress’ because there are two clear camps: large, flagship events and meetings run by larger organizations (likely because they have event teams deep enough in staffing to do so). Smaller companies and most associations understand the power of their apps’ backend analytics and what it reveals about attendee behavior, but they often lack the bandwidth to dig in. It’s up to app vendors like us to make analytics even more accessible and consumable. This means more than just automatic generation of pretty pre-packaged charts and reports; app vendors need to spend time with event planners to understand their success metrics, and then design analytics reports that speak directly to them.

Prediction #6: The proliferation of mobile event app vendors will continue

Achieved

No surprise here, as a growing market attracts more vendors. 2014 saw no consolidation or acquisitions in the event app space, but saw still more vendors enter the market as the pie grew. There were no dramatic shifts in market share or positioning as all the recognized incumbents continued their march where they left off.

Prediction #7: The emergence of genuine ‘connected communities’

In Progress

As we stated above as part of the outcome for Prediction 3, more event planners than ever began using their event apps beyond the closing remarks. Here at QuickMobile, we saw growing adoption of what we call ‘advanced containers’ – adding regular app functionality at the multi-event container level to share content, sustain conversations and continue to give attendees reasons to use the app all year long. That said, perhaps the biggest barrier to the emergence of ‘connected communities’ is that often, it’s not the job of the event planner to make this happen, and they’re too busy moving on to the next big event to even think about it. We believe there is still tremendous potential to create connected communities through an event app; it just takes planning and resources.
